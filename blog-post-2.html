How to Navigate Your First 90 Days at a New Job

The first three months at a Fortune 500 company can make or break your trajectory. Here's what hiring managers actually want to see.

I've watched thousands of new hires start at major corporations, and the difference between those who thrive and those who struggle often comes down to how they approach their first 90 days.

After managing teams at Microsoft, IBM, and Kodak, I can tell you exactly what separates high performers from those who just get by. Most new employees make the same critical mistakes in their first quarter—mistakes that can limit their opportunities for years.

Week 1-2: Listen More Than You Talk

Your first instinct might be to prove yourself immediately by sharing ideas and demonstrating expertise. Resist that urge.

The most successful new hires I've seen spend their first two weeks in learning mode. They ask thoughtful questions, take detailed notes, and most importantly—they observe the unwritten rules of how work actually gets done.

Schedule one-on-ones with key stakeholders. Not to pitch ideas, but to understand their priorities, challenges, and how your role intersects with theirs. Ask: "What does success look like for our team in the next six months?" and "What's the biggest challenge you're facing right now?"

Week 3-6: Deliver Quick Wins

By week three, you should have identified 2-3 small but meaningful projects you can deliver quickly. These aren't your major initiatives—they're strategic wins that build credibility.

At Microsoft, I saw new Program Managers succeed by fixing a broken process or automating a manual task within their first month. These weren't glamorous projects, but they demonstrated initiative and delivered immediate value.

Focus on wins that are: visible to your manager, solve a real pain point, and can be completed within 3-4 weeks. Document your impact with metrics whenever possible.

Week 7-12: Build Your Network Strategically

By month two, you should understand the political landscape. Now it's time to build relationships with people who can help you succeed long-term.

Identify the influencers—not just the executives, but the people who consistently get things done. These are your cross-functional partners, subject matter experts, and the employees everyone respects.

Offer to help before asking for anything. Volunteer for cross-team initiatives. Be the person who follows through reliably on commitments.

The 90-Day Review: What Your Manager Is Actually Evaluating

At the three-month mark, your manager is assessing whether hiring you was the right decision. They're looking for three things: technical competence, cultural fit, and growth potential.

Before your 90-day review, prepare a brief summary of what you've accomplished, what you've learned, and where you need support. Be honest about challenges—managers respect self-awareness far more than false confidence.

Most importantly, come with specific ideas for how you can increase your impact in the next quarter. This demonstrates you're thinking beyond just "getting through" the probation period.
